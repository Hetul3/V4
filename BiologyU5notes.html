Parts of the Respiratory System

The respiratory system has the major function of performing gas exchange so that your body can recieve the oxygen it needs and remove CO2 it doesn't. It also has the minor function of defending your body against microogranisms in the respiratory tract, producing sound, and assisting in the control of body fluid pH.

The respiratory system is divided into two sections, the upper tract and lower tract.

Upper Tract:

Nose/Nasal Cavity:
- Hairs filter dust and particles
- Tissues line the nasal cavity to moisten and warm the air
- Capillaries in the nose that when ruptured can cause nose bleed
- Preferred for inhalation

Pharynx:
- Combines mouth and nose
- Splits into the trachea for air (Nasopharynx) and the esophagus (Oropharynx)

Larynx:
- Voice box
- Epiglottis covers it
- Prevents food from liquids from entering into lungs
- Where the vocal cords are


Lower Tract:

Trachea:
- Long pipe that leads to the lungs
- Windpipe
- Cartilage covers the trachea
Bronchi:
- Divides trachea into two into each lung

Bronchioles:
- Splits of bronchi that keeps splitting until it becomes ends where it contains alveoli

Alveoli:
- There are 300 million alveoli
- The point of them is to increase surface area for gas exchange
- They are covered in capillaries that can allow for oxygen to transfer into the blood and CO2 from the lungs to go into the alveoli
- The air needs to be moist for the diffusion to occur

Lungs:
- One of the largest organs of the body
- Has ribs to cover it and protect it, the ribs are connected by the intercostal muscles which expand and contract
- There is the diaphragm underneat which is basically pure muscle, it contracts and allows air to be drawn into the lungs, when relaxed it forces air out of the lungs
- The membrane that surronds the lungs is known as the pleural membrane

Gas Exchange and Respiration

We should all be familiar to the concept of respiration, I mean, it's the process that the whole organ system is named after. Basically, it's the physical process of moving air in and out of the lungs and the body. This is done through the use of the lungs, but mainly it is done with the muscles surrounding the lungs; those being the diaphragm, intercostal muscles, and the thing that controls it all, the medulla oblongata.

To get a better understanding of each, we need to know the fact that the lungs cannot contract and expand on their own. Remember, they're an organ without any muscle tissue so the brain cannot control the contraction and expansion of it, instead when the lung actually expands, it's because of the air pressure from the air filling it.

This is due to the said muscles. When the diaphragm contracts, the lungs will have extra space at the bottom where they can go in and fill up. Ontop of this, the intercostal muscles between your ribs will also relax and expand the rib cage to allow the lungs to grow, this is the process of exhalation.

The complete opposite happens when you inhale. The diaphragm and intercostal muscles will contract and the lungs will push the air out since there isn't room for them to go.

Now, all of this is controlled by the part of the brain known as the medulla oblongata. It is responsible for the unconcious breathing where you breathe without thinking about it (you are now probably are thinking of manually breathing and that is controlled by the frontal cortex).

When talking about gas exchange and what the process is. It represents the oxygen molecules and CO2 molecules physically being transferred between the atmosphere and the red bood cells in the blood.

Like many important processes, this all happens in the lungs, or specifically the alveolis which are surrounded by by capillaries. The walls between the alveoli and the capillaries are thin enough that gases and molecules can directly diffuse between them. The way that they diffuse is through the phenemenom known as partial pressures.

What are partial pressures you ask? It works exactly like gas laws but is specific to the type of molecules that are being transferred. Gas will tend to move from an area of high pressure to an area of low pressure to make it so that the whole region is equally balanced in these pressures. Partial pressure uses the same principle, but is specific to the type of gas. So, you may have two regions, one is pressured and the other isn't, but the area that is pressured may only have a little amount of gas x, while the area that is low pressured has a higher concentration of gas x. After letting both of them interact. The low pressured area will have gas rush into it, but gas x will actually flow out of it to make the concentration of it even. So it doesn't matter about if the blood is more or less pressured than the atmosphere, all that matters is concentration of the important molecules. In this case, oxygen is 13.3kPa concentrated in the atmosphere, while the concentration of oxygen in deoxygenated blood is 5.33kPa, so the oxygen will rush in to supply that region. The opposite is true for carbon dioxide as there is a higher concentration in the blood than the atmosphere so it rushes out.

This is the same process that helps oxygen diffuse into your muscles and carbon dioxide to diffuse into your blood when the blood is near the muscles.

Other Respiratory Systems

While we humans use lungs to breath, many other animals have no need of that. The main example is of course gills in fish, and lungs were evolved to help animals exist in the terrestrial environment outside of the water where oxygen is more abundant. However, even then it's not the case that every animal in land has lungs as you'll soon see.


Gills:
Gills employ countercurrent flow to the water to grab as much oxygen from the water as they can. They still use the idea of gas exchange and partial pressures, but are a bit more clever about it. As water goes from the front of the fish to the back, the gills will diffuse more oxygen and lowering the oxygen concentration of water making it harder for the oxygen to diffuse through. In this case, blood will move counter to the current of the water, so that they can always have a favourable graident with the water and always be extracting oxygen from it.



Amphibians:
Amphibian and specifically frog skin in some species is actually thin enough that diffuse can occur directly. So their whole skin and body acts like their lungs where the blood just needs to go to the surface of the skin to get oxygen, this is one of the reasons why frogs don't require 4 chambers and only have 3 since they don't need to pump deoxygentated blood to the lungs.



Insects:
The respiratory system of insects is different due to the fact that they have an ope circulatory system in contrast to our own closed system. In our system, blood is always in blood vessels, while in an open system, the blood can be directly laid onto muscles without a layer of blood vessels, this means that their diffusion is much more direct and the blood can soak in the muscles.
